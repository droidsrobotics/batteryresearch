The Battery Life Cycle

Learn about all the stages in the battery recycling process from manufacturing all the way through collecting the raw materials from a used battery.

  • Manufacturing

    Energizer, Duracell, Panasonic

    We talked to leading battery manufacturers to learn about issues they face. Majority of batteries manufactured are still alkalines. Since they no longer contain mercury, they are not harmful for the environment. We learned about Energizer's EcoAdvanced Battery (4% reused battery material). We also learned about how battery manufacturers are pushing for legislation in the US. Currently, only in California and Vermont are there laws about recycling one-time use batteries.

  • Consumption

    You and Me

    We did a survey of 250 people and contacted FLL teams around the world to ask them about their battery recycling habits. The results were suprising. People in the US are not aware of what the battery recycling laws are in their state, and find it very hard. In Europe and South America, there are a lot more collection locations for all types of batteries (alkaline and rechargeable) compared to the US.

  • Collection

    Call2Recycle, TerraCycle

    After talking to Call2Recycle and TerraCycle, we learned about paid and free used battery collection systems. We learned that battery manufacturers cover the cost of the collection for Call2Recycle. We visited collection locations at Best Buy, Home Depot and Staples and discovered that the collection boxes are sometimes hidden and that people rarely follow the rules (place alkalines in a rechargeable-only box). Collection locations in the US are not in places people visit on a daily basis (like a school, grocery store or street corner).

  • Sorting

    Battery Solutions

    We visited two of the largest battery recyclers in the world. Both places start with batteries in boxes or buckets. They sort the batteries by hand into different chemistries - a slow and expensive process. Battery Solutions does have the only battery sorting machine in US (by Refind) for large volume containers, but this machine has its limitations. Sorting is 1/3 of the total cost of recycling a battery and recyclers include a fee for this step.

  • Processing

    Inmetco

    We saw each stage of the process in recovering the raw materials. We learned about the costs for sorting and processing batteries. The zinc, manganese and steel are all recovered and used in other industries. The raw materials from an alkaline battery are not worth much, so recyclers charge fees to sort and process the alkalines.

  • Reuse

    Inmetco, Battery Solutions

    Battery Solutions and Inmetco taught us about what happens next with the raw materials. Some of it is used back in batteries. The rest is used in other industries, (e.g. steel, brick, and fertilizer).

Big Problems in Battery Recycling

80% of all batteries manufactured are alkalines and 90% of all batteries end up in US landfills. U.S. consumers don't recycle batteries enough!


Increase Used Battery Collection

There needs to be more battery collection locations in the United States. Adding them to schools, grocery stores, libraries and street corners are important. Several of the FLL teams we partnered with are working on this problem.

Reduce the number of alkalines used

Encouraging consumers to use rechargeables is also a great step in reducing the number of discarded batteries. Another one of our FLL friends is working on this problem.

Reduce the number of batteries discarded

Provide ways to make sure the batteries are completely spent (used). Battery Solutions informed us that many batteries tend to have charge left in them. Finding a way to use this energy is a great idea.

The Bigger Problem!

The raw materials in alkalines are not worth the cost to recycle them. So the big question is...

What are the costs to recycle batteries and how do you reduce them?

Battery Sorting

Sorting is done by hand!

Step 1 Workers have to be hired and trained to identify different batteries by chemistry.

Step 2 Workers cut open boxes or open buckets of batteries. They sort them into all the different chemistries (6+ chemistries) by hand.

Step 3 Workers track each type of battery so that they can provide this information to the person who sent them the used batteries.

With ReFind (only at Battery Solutions):

Step 1 Workers start with larger drums of batteries. They have to hand sort the batteries to separate cylindrical and 9V batteries from the rest.

Step 2 The sorted batteries are loaded into the Refind machine to sort again.

Step 3 The machine will sort the battery if it has an image of it. Otherwise, it will reject the battery. The machine will also record the brand, size, type, etc.

Invisi-Sort

The Invisi-Sort is desgined to address key problems faced in the battery industry.

1) Manufacturers we spoke to are willing to print something on the battery to identify the chemistry. Printing is a minimal cost to them. However, they want to keep their brand image. Therefore, we will identify the battery chemistries with invisible ink (UV paint).

2) Processors want machines to accept more sizes/shapes. By using a wider conveyor belt and a camera sensor that can detect multiple batteries at once, we can accept more sizes. This will also increase the speed of the machine.

3) Sorters need to provide customers with data on batteries. We can make small-scale machines that can sort at each sorting station. Combined with a system like Refind, we can still provide this data to customers and eliminate hand sorting even for individual boxes and buckets.
